Fundamental

Magic Number — Sales Efficiency

Source: Baird Equity Research, April 26, 2022 · Shi Priority: Quick Intro

The Magic Number is a SaaS metric measuring how efficiently a company converts Sales & Marketing spend into recurring revenue. It approximates the revenue generated (on a trailing twelve-month basis) for every dollar spent on S&M in the preceding cycle.

Why It Matters

Magic Number helps you answer: "Should this company be investing more or less in sales?" A high number means each S&M dollar is generating outsized revenue — you should pour fuel on the fire. A low number means the go-to-market engine is inefficient and needs to be fixed before scaling.

Formula — Short Sales Cycles (< 90 days / PLG models)
Magic Number = (Quarterly Revenuet - Quarterly Revenuet-1) × 4 ÷ S&M Spendt-1 → Then take trailing 4-quarter average
Formula — Long Sales Cycles (Enterprise, > 90 days)
Magic Number = (TTM Revenuet - TTM Revenuet-1) ÷ Prior Year S&M Spend

Baird revised the formula in 2022 because the original quarterly metric didn't account for longer sales/deployment cycles. For enterprise companies, the revenue impact of S&M investment takes longer to show up.

Magic Number Interpretation Action Signal
> 1.0 Highly efficient S&M — strong ROI on each dollar spent Aggressively increase S&M investment
0.75 – 1.0 Good efficiency — investment is paying off Continue investing; monitor trends
< 0.75 Inefficient — more spent than revenue generated Fix S&M efficiency before scaling spend
Shi's Takeaway

The Magic Number is another lens on sales efficiency alongside LTV/CAC. The key difference: Magic Number is backwards-looking (what did past S&M spend produce?) while LTV/CAC is forward-looking (what is the expected lifetime value of a new customer?). Use both together.

Unprofitable ≠ Uninvestable — How Accounting Distorts Profit Views

Source: Needham Industry Update, April 26, 2022 · Shi Priority: Crash Course on Company Valuation
The Core Argument

Company valuation = PV of all future cash flows, NOT PV of future earnings. GAAP losses in SaaS are largely an accounting artifact driven by FASB rules that force software companies to expense growth investments (R&D, S&M headcount) as incurred — while companies in retail, industrials, and energy can capitalize those same investments. Apples-to-oranges comparison.

The Walmart vs. Workday Case Study

Walmart (WMT) — Traditional Retail
  • GAAP profitable at IPO (Year 8)
  • Did NOT generate positive FCF until Year 34
  • Growth investments = store buildings (capitalized on balance sheet, not expensed)
  • CapEx peaked at 5% of revenue during growth years
  • FCF margins never surpassed 5% over its history
Workday (WDAY) — Subscription SaaS
  • Generated positive FCF in Year 10
  • Did NOT generate GAAP net income until Year 16
  • Growth investments = R&D + S&M headcount (expensed immediately)
  • FCF margins better than any year in WMT's history since 2015
  • SBC-adjusted FCF still outperformed WMT by 2021
Key Accounting Insight

If you expensed Walmart's CapEx as incurred (like FASB does for software R&D), WMT would have looked deeply unprofitable for its first 20+ years — yet no one would call WMT a bad business. The same logic applies in reverse to SaaS: the forced in-period expensing makes SaaS look worse than it is during the growth phase.

The Tax Efficiency Bonus

Because SaaS companies expense growth investments immediately, they accumulate large Net Operating Loss (NOL) carryforwards. This means:

Phase 1: Growth
Heavy R&D/S&M expenses → GAAP losses → NOL accumulates
→
Phase 2: Maturity
NOLs shield taxable income → delayed cash tax payments
→
Result
Lower PV of tax liabilities than traditional businesses (Time Value of Money)

Critical Insight from DCF Analysis

Valuation multiples are far more sensitive to revenue growth duration than near-term profitability. A company sustaining 40% growth for 8 years (17.55x) is worth 2.8× more than one that grows for only 4 years (6.21x) — regardless of near-term margins. Rule of 40 — The North Star Metric

Source: Barclays (referenced in reading list) · Shi Priority: North Star Metric for Software Companies

The Rule of 40 is the dominant benchmark metric for evaluating whether a SaaS company is striking the right balance between growth and profitability. It recognizes that early-stage SaaS companies must trade off margin for growth.

Why This Metric Matters for PE/Investors

The Rule of 40 is the primary benchmark used in investor conversations and company pitches. A company at Rule of 40 can justify its S&M and R&D spend. In Needham's DCF framework, they assume that for each growth tier, operating margins conform to Rule of 40 (e.g., 40% growth companies operate at ~0% margins; 50% growth at -10% margins). This is the "normal" in SaaS.

Fundamental

LTV / CAC — Customer Return on Investment

Source: Goldman Sachs (referenced in reading list) · Shi Priority: Most Important Sales Efficiency Metric

LTV/CAC is Shi's pick for the single most important sales efficiency metric. It measures the return on investment from acquiring a new customer — how much lifetime value does each customer generate relative to what it cost to acquire them?

LTV — Lifetime Value
Revenue × Gross Margin ÷ Churn Rate
The total gross profit expected from a customer over their lifetime. Higher gross margins and lower churn = higher LTV. This is why SaaS businesses obsess over net revenue retention.
CAC — Customer Acquisition Cost
S&M Spend ÷ New Customers Added
The total sales and marketing cost to acquire one new customer. Includes sales headcount, marketing programs, commissions. CAC payback = how long until a customer becomes profitable.
LTV/CAC Ratio
LTV / CAC = (Gross Margin % × ARPU) / Churn Rate ÷ S&M Spend / New Customers Added Simplified: Annual Contract Value × Gross Margin % × Customer Lifetime Years ÷ Cost to Acquire One Customer
LTV/CAC Ratio Interpretation
> 3x Excellent — SaaS benchmark for a healthy, scalable business
1x – 3x Acceptable but room to improve — typically early-stage or in heavy growth investment
< 1x Destroying value — costs more to acquire a customer than they'll ever generate
Connection to Magic Number

Magic Number and LTV/CAC are both sales efficiency metrics but measure different things. Magic Number is a quick, market-observable approximation using public financials (revenue growth ÷ S&M spend). LTV/CAC requires deeper data: churn rates, gross margins per customer, and acquisition costs broken out by channel. Together they triangulate the health of the go-to-market engine.

ARR / Billings / RPO (RBC — Not Covered in Detail Here)

Shi also flagged the RBC ARR/Billings/RPO note for topline metrics. Key definitions: ARR = Annual Recurring Revenue (snapshot of recurring revenue at a point in time). Billings = Revenue + change in deferred revenue (approximates bookings). RPO = Remaining Performance Obligation (contracted revenue not yet recognized — forward-looking). Billings and RPO are important leading indicators; they show booked demand before it hits the income statement.

Fundamental

ARR, Billings & RPO — Topline Leading Indicators

Shi's Direction

From the RBC Software Primers report: "Focus on topline metrics; think you can ignore the company-specific analyses." The key concept here is that SaaS revenue is backward-looking — understanding billings, RPO, and ARR is how investors (and analysts) look ahead. These are the primary leading indicators of future growth.

Why Leading Indicators Matter in SaaS

In a SaaS model, GAAP revenue is a lagging metric. Roughly 63% of a typical SaaS company's quarterly revenue was already sitting in deferred revenue at the start of the quarter — meaning it reflects decisions customers made months or years ago, not what's happening now. To gauge current business momentum, you need forward-looking signals.

Calculated Billings
Revenue + Δ Deferred Revenue
The legacy "gold standard" leading indicator. Derived entirely from GAAP line items — no management judgment required. Measures new business invoiced in the period. Increasingly unreliable due to duration sensitivity, consumption model incompatibility, and potential for manipulation.
RPO (Remaining Performance Obligations)
Total committed future revenue
A GAAP metric introduced under ASC 606. Captures all future obligations from signed contracts — both billed (deferred revenue) and unbilled backlog. More comprehensive than billings because it includes multi-year contract backlog that never touches deferred revenue until invoiced.
ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue)
Annualized value of active subscriptions
A non-GAAP metric — each company defines it slightly differently. Most common method: annualize the most recent quarter's recurring revenue (Q × 4). Most useful for cloud-transition companies and consumption-heavy businesses where GAAP revenue timing distorts the picture.

The Three-Metric Framework — When to Use Each

MetricGAAP?Best ForMain Weakness
Billings Derived from GAAP Pure-play SaaS with annual billing cycles and stable durations Sensitive to billing duration; useless for consumption companies; can be gamed
RPO / cRPO Yes Enterprise SaaS with multi-year contracts; companies that guide to RPO (CRM, NOW, WDAY) Some companies apply the ASC 606 "practical expedient" and exclude short contracts, making it incomplete
ARR No — non-GAAP Cloud-transition companies; consumption models; private companies at growth stage No standard definition — Snowflake, CrowdStrike, and Dropbox all calculate it differently

Key Formulas

Calculated Billings = Revenue + (Ending Deferred Revenue – Beginning Deferred Revenue) Current Billings (ST) = Revenue + Change in Short-Term Deferred Revenue only [better: controls for duration shifts] Total RPO = Billed backlog (deferred revenue) + Unbilled contractual backlog Current RPO (cRPO) = Portion of total RPO expected to be recognized in next 12 months RPO Coverage Ratio = cRPO ÷ NTM Revenue [RBC median across SaaS: ~63–66%] Calculated Bookings = Revenue + Change in Total RPO [RPO-era equivalent of billings] ARR (common estimate) = Most recent quarter's subscription revenue × 4 ARR (CrowdStrike) = Annualized value of active subscription contracts at period end ARR (Clearwater) = (Last month's recurring revenue ÷ days in month) × 365
The "Death of Billings" — Why This Matters

Billings was the dominant SaaS metric for years because it could be calculated from standard GAAP financials without needing management disclosure. But COVID exposed its fragility: customers shifted to monthly/quarterly billing cycles, collapsing billings figures even at healthy companies. ASC 606's introduction of RPO gave investors a better tool. Billings is now considered noisy, not conclusive — RBC's recommendation is to use RPO or cRPO for enterprise SaaS, and ARR for transition/consumption stories. For OneStream's customer contracts in Kantata, these same distinctions apply: the timing and structure of how deals are invoiced affects which metric most accurately captures backlog health.

Accounting

Why Companies Transition to SaaS

Source: Jefferies SaaS Transition (referenced in reading list) · Needham Profitability Note · Shi Priority: Explains the transition rationale

Understanding the SaaS transition is foundational — it explains why software companies shifted away from perpetual licenses, and why the resulting accounting changes create the "unprofitable" optics that Needham debunks above.

For Vendors
Predictable, Recurring Revenue
Subscription revenue recognized ratably smooths lumpy perpetual license deals. Predictable ARR enables better planning, supports higher valuation multiples, and reduces reliance on year-end deal closings.
For Customers
Lower UpFront Cost, Always Current
No large upfront CapEx — OpEx model. Always on the latest version. Easier to expand usage. Exit costs are lower (though this cuts both ways for vendor retention).
Long-Term Business Quality
Compounding Renewal Economics
Renewals require no incremental S&M cost. As the installed base grows, renewals become a larger share of revenue. Net Revenue Retention above 100% = organic growth from existing customers alone.
The Accounting Consequence of SaaS

Under legacy perpetual license models, revenue was front-loaded (recognized when software was delivered). Under SaaS, revenue is recognized ratably over the contract term. This means a company switching from perpetual to SaaS will see apparent revenue decline during transition — even though underlying bookings and customer value may be growing. This is not a business deterioration; it's an accounting timing shift. Investors must look at ARR and Billings, not just GAAP revenue, to see what's happening.

ASC 606 — Revenue Recognition Mechanics

Source: SailPoint ASC 606 Summary · Shi Priority: How bookings/billings turn into revenue

ASC 606 is the FASB revenue recognition standard that replaced ASC 605. The key change for software companies: term licenses are no longer recognized ratably — the license element is front-loaded upon delivery, while maintenance/support is spread over the contract period. This matters for modeling because it accelerates revenue recognition for certain deal types.

Commission Expense Changes Under ASC 606

One of the less-discussed changes: sales commissions are now capitalized and amortized over the estimated customer life (~5 years) rather than expensed in the period of the deal. This improves near-term margins but creates a deferred commission asset on the balance sheet.

Deal Type ASC 605 (Old Commission Treatment) ASC 606 (New Treatment)
Perpetual License Expensed fully upon deal close License portion at close; rest amortized over 5-year customer life
Term License Expensed ratably over contract term License portion at close; rest amortized over 5-year customer life
SaaS Expensed ratably over contract term Amortized over estimated 5-year customer life (longer period)
Term License Illustration — The Dramatic Impact

SailPoint's example shows this clearly: a $300 three-year term license with standard support generates $100/year for 3 years under ASC 605, but $225 in Year 1 + $38/year in Years 2-3 under ASC 606. The same total economics, but radically different income statement timing. When comparing software companies across eras, always check which standard they're reporting under.

Risks

Putting It All Together — Shi's Framework

Step 1 — Quality check: Is the business model sound? Look at gross margins (≥75% for pure SaaS) and NRR (is the existing customer base growing?).

Step 2 — Growth check: What's driving topline growth — new logos (Magic Number, CAC payback) or expansion within base (NRR)? Both matter.

Step 3 — Efficiency check: Rule of 40 — is the combination of growth and profitability strong enough to justify the investment?

Step 4 — Valuation: Based on the growth rate and its expected duration, what EV/Revenue multiple is justified? Use the Needham DCF framework as a sanity check. GAAP profitability is almost irrelevant — FCF trajectory and unit economics are what matter.

Jefferies — No Pain, No Gain: The SaaS Transition Playbook

Source: Jefferies Equity Research, February 28, 2022 — Charles Brennan & Alex Nguyen · Shi Priority: Explains why companies like to go to SaaS
Shi's Framing

This note is categorized under Accounting Knowledge in your reading list — specifically to explain why companies transition to SaaS. But the Jefferies analysis goes deeper: it also documents the financial J-curve (the "pain") and the long-term valuation re-rating (the "gain"), making it essential for investor conversations about any software vendor mid-transition. OneStream itself is a SaaS company — this framework directly applies.

What Is a SaaS Transition?

Jefferies identifies two simultaneous changes when a software company goes SaaS:

1. Architectural Shift
  • Software moves from on-premise (installed in customer systems, managed by customer) to cloud-hosted (private or public cloud, managed by the vendor)
  • Vendor controls updates, uptime, and version management
  • All customers can be kept on the same version — enabling faster product innovation cycles
  • Drives operational gains: scalability, flexibility, cross-sell, infrastructure monetization
2. Contracting Shift
  • From licence + maintenance (large upfront licence fee + ~20% annual maintenance) to subscription (bundled, recognized ratably over contract)
  • Revenue recognition pushed to the right — same total value, but spread over years instead of front-loaded at signing
  • This creates the J-curve: near-term revenue compression, eventual acceleration as the subscription base compounds
  • Management rarely cuts costs during revenue dip, causing temporary margin compression

The 5 Operational Gains

1. Scalability
Cloud scales with demand
No need to buy infrastructure for peak capacity. Elastic compute is more cost-efficient than on-prem hardware provisioning.
2. Flexibility
Access from anywhere
Cloud enables remote work, mobile access, and easier collaboration — directly improving customer satisfaction and stickiness.
3. Automatic Updates
Vendor manages versions
All customers on the same version = faster feature delivery, fewer support tickets for legacy bugs, more innovation cycles per year.
4. Cross-Selling
Seamless expansion
Cloud delivery makes it frictionless for customers to adopt additional modules, driving higher NRR and revenue per customer.
5. Infrastructure Revenue
Capture hosting costs
Vendors can charge holistically for the solution including infrastructure. They use hyperscalers (AWS/Azure/GCP) at volume discounts — the spread is incremental low-risk revenue.

The Quantified Gain — Jefferies' Cross-Company Data

Jefferies studied 4 companies that completed SaaS transitions (ADSK, PTC, ADBE, INTU) plus 4 mid-transition (SAP, Sage, Software AG, CyberArk). Key results from the completers:

Revenue Growth Acceleration
+6 percentage points
Average acceleration in annual revenue growth rate post-transition vs. pre-transition. Growth re-accelerates once subscription base compounds and licence drag disappears.
Margin Improvement
+250 bps avg. EBIT
Recurring revenue provides operating leverage — cost base becomes more fixed relative to revenue. Compared to pre-transition adj. EBIT margins.
Valuation Re-Rating
+~3x EV/NTM Revenue
All 4 studied US completers saw meaningful multiple expansion 2 years after margins inflected. Market rewards recurring revenue quality and visible growth.
Average Transition Duration
3.7 years average
Range: ADSK took only 1 year; INTU took nearly 6 years. Depends on product complexity, starting mix of recurring revenue, and how aggressively management pushes the migration.

The Cost — The J-Curve in Detail

Revenue Impact
  • Near-term revenue growth falls by ~10% as licence revenue (which was front-loaded) gives way to ratable subscription revenue
  • In extreme cases (like Adobe in 2013), GAAP revenue can go negative year-over-year during a fast transition
  • This is an accounting artifact — ARR and bookings may be growing strongly throughout
  • Once licence revenue shrinks to a negligible share (~10-15%), subscription compounding takes over and revenue growth accelerates
Margin Impact
  • Companies increase opex by ~5% on average to invest in cloud infrastructure and retrain/restructure sales for subscription GTM
  • Average margin compression: ~15% from peak to trough
  • Average quarters to margin trough: ~6 quarters
  • Average quarters to return to pre-transition margin level: ~9 quarters
  • Total foregone profit (transition "cost"): average ~1.5 years of pre-transition profit
Key Inflection Point: ~75-80% Recurring Revenue

Once a company reaches roughly 75–80% recurring revenue as a share of total revenue, Jefferies found that margins typically start inflecting upward. At this point, the legacy licence revenue is small enough that it no longer drags on reported growth figures, and the subscription base's compounding economics dominate. This is the signal investors watch for when evaluating mid-transition companies.

As of February 2022, Jefferies noted that all 4 completed US transitions resulted in positive valuation re-ratings, but there was no evidence of valuation upside in Europe. SAP, Sage, and Software AG were all still trading at discounts to their pre-transition levels. Jefferies speculated this may reflect different investor bases, market depth, or product complexity factors — but it was still unresolved. This is a useful reminder that macro/market context matters alongside fundamentals.

This is a competitive advantage: OneStream entered the market with all the operational benefits of SaaS (scalability, continuous updates, subscription economics) without the J-curve penalty that legacy ERPs like SAP and Oracle are still navigating. Goldman Sachs — Benchmarking LTV:CAC Across the Software Sector

Source: Goldman Sachs Equity Research, March 8, 2022 — Gabriela Borges, Kash Rangan et al. · Shi Priority: Focus on LTV:CAC — "Most Important Sales Efficiency Metric"
Shi's Direction

Shi flagged this as the primary source for understanding LTV:CAC. The Goldman report covers all major software KPIs for Q4 2021, but the key takeaways for you are: (1) how LTV and CAC are formally defined and calculated, (2) the broader market context shift from "growth at any cost" to "profitable growth," and (3) how LTV:CAC correlates to EV/Sales multiples.

Goldman's Formal LTV:CAC Definitions

Goldman's methodology is worth understanding precisely — it's how this metric is tracked at the institutional level:

LTV Formula (Goldman)
LTV = GAAP Subscription Gross Margin % ÷ (Gross Revenue Churn % + 8.5% discount rate) Key inputs: • Gross margin: cost to service $1 of recurring revenue • Gross churn: how fast the customer base decays • 8.5% discount rate: reflects long-term displacement/ disruption risk inherent in software

The 8.5% discount rate is important — it acknowledges that even great SaaS businesses face competitive risk over time. LTV is the discounted present value of $1 of recurring revenue.

CAC Formula (Goldman)
CAC = TTM GAAP S&M Expense ÷ (Gross New ARR) Where: • GAAP S&M includes stock-based compensation (Goldman views SBC as a real cost of doing business) • Gross New ARR = YoY change in ARR + gross churn (measures cost to add $1 of gross new ARR, before churn)

Using gross new ARR (not net) is deliberate — it isolates the new logo acquisition cost from the churn offset, giving a cleaner view of sales engine efficiency.

Q4 2021 Market Context — Why LTV:CAC Became More Important

The Rate Shift
Growth at any cost is over
Rising interest rates in 2022 compressed software multiples back to 2019 levels (average EV/sales/growth multiple of 0.38x). Investors shifted focus from pure revenue growth to quality of growth — specifically, unit economics and FCF generation.
Correlation Shift
LTV:CAC ↑, Revenue Growth ↓
Goldman found that in Q4 2021, the correlation of EV/Sales multiples with LTV:CAC increased while the correlation with revenue growth decreased. The market was rewarding efficiency, not just speed.
Median LTV:CAC

Barclays — The Rule of 40 Handbook

Source: Barclays Equity Research, April 4, 2022 — Raimo Lenschow CFA et al. · Shi Priority: "Great introduction to many software companies' North Star metric"
Shi's Framing

Shi called this a "great introduction to the Rule of 40." The Barclays handbook goes well beyond the basic formula — it covers the nuances of which profitability metric to use (EBITDA vs. Operating Profit vs. FCF), five factors that affect the calculation, and a "Modified Rule of 40" that better correlates to valuation. This is the most technical of the five fundamental readings.

Origin and Core Concept

The Rule of 40 was popularized in two 2015 blog posts by VCs Brad Feld and Fred Wilson, who had heard a late-stage investor describe the "40% rule for a healthy software company." The formula: Revenue Growth % + Profitability Margin % ≥ 40%. A SaaS startup with 60% growth and –20% FCF margin hits 40; a mature software company with 20% growth and 20% FCF margin also hits 40. The metric explicitly normalizes for different life-stage tradeoffs.

The Definition Problem — Not All Rule of 40s Are Equal

Barclays' most important finding: the same company can have Rule of 40 scores that vary by up to 25 percentage points depending on which profitability metric you use. This is not a minor rounding difference — it fundamentally changes the investment conclusion in some cases.

Why EBITDA is Highest
  • Adds back D&A, which is large for capital-intensive companies and those with M&A goodwill/intangibles
  • Adds back stock-based compensation (in adjusted versions)
  • Benefits from deferred commission amortization policies (longer = better EBITDA)
  • Result: can significantly overstate true cash generation ability
Why FCF is Best (Barclays' Recommendation)
  • Accounts for capital intensity — companies like Oracle (DigitalOcean manage own data centers) get appropriately penalized
  • Accounts for leverage — high interest expense reduces FCF but not EBITDA
  • Reflects actual cash generation available to investors
  • Barclays found FCF-based Rule of 40 has highest R² correlation to EV/Sales multiples (52% vs. 36% for EBITDA)
Extreme Examples from Barclays' Data

DigitalOcean: Rule of 40 using EBITDA = 67% vs. FCF = 40% — a 27-point gap driven by heavy capex (own data centers). Oracle: EBITDA = 56% vs. FCF = 30% — 26-point gap from litigation charges and capital intensity. Paycom: EBITDA = 65% vs. FCF = 44% — 21-point gap partly from a 10-year deferred commission amortization period (vs. industry standard 3–5 years), which inflates operating profit.

5 Factors That Affect Rule of 40 Interpretation

Factor 1
Deployment Model / Subscription Transition
Companies mid-transition see Rule of 40 compressed (both revenue growth and margins dip simultaneously). Adobe's Rule of 40 fell from ~49% to 15% during its 2012–2014 transition — use ARR growth instead of revenue growth for mid-transition companies to get a cleaner view.
Factor 2
Capital Intensity
Companies managing own infrastructure (MSFT, ORCL, DOCN) have material capex that depresses FCF vs. EBITDA. For pure-play SaaS companies using hyperscalers, capex is minimal and the delta between metrics is smaller.
Factor 3
M&A — D&A Inflation
Post-acquisition intangible amortization flows through D&A — inflating the EBITDA vs. operating profit gap. Companies like Coupa, Lightspeed, and Descartes have high M&A D&A. FCF sidesteps this issue better than EBITDA.
Factor 4
Leverage Profile
High debt → high interest expense → lower FCF but no impact on EBITDA. When comparing companies with materially different capital structures, consider using unlevered FCF in the Rule of 40 for a cleaner operational comparison.
Factor 5
Deferred Commission Amortization
Longer amortization periods (like Paycom's 10 years vs. typical 3–5 years) reduce recognized S&M expense in the near term, artificially boosting operating margins. This inflates EBITDA-based Rule of 40. FCF captures the actual cash paid.

RBC Capital Markets — ARR, Billings, or RPO: What's the Best Leading Indicator?

Source: RBC Capital Markets Equity Research, April 28, 2022 — Rishi Jaluria, Matthew Hedberg et al. · Shi Priority: "Focus on topline metrics; ignore the company-specific analyses (page 14+)"
Shi's Direction — What to Take Away

This is RBC's fifth software primer in an ongoing series covering key topics in SaaS analysis. Shi flagged it specifically for topline metrics — ARR, billings, and RPO are how investors look forward when GAAP revenue is inherently backward-looking. The 73 company-by-company analyses starting on page 14 are not the point. The point is understanding why each metric exists, what it measures, where it breaks down, and when investors prefer one over another. This framework applies directly to reading any SaaS company's earnings — and to understanding what the contract data you manage in Kantata actually means at the investor level.

Why Leading Indicators Are Essential in SaaS

In a SaaS model, revenue is recognized ratably over the contract term — meaning quarterly GAAP revenue primarily reflects business booked in prior periods, not what's happening now. RBC quantifies this two ways:

The Deferred Revenue % Signal
Median SaaS company: 63% of Q revenue from prior deferred
For the most recent Q1 in RBC's coverage, the median non-consumption SaaS company recognized 63% of its subscription revenue from deferred revenue that was already on the balance sheet at the start of the quarter. That 63% reflects decisions made in prior periods — not current business momentum. Some companies (Coupa, Dynatrace, Zscaler) are above 90%. For companies where this percentage is significantly lower, billings becomes less relevant as a metric because less revenue is rolling through deferred in the first place.
RPO Coverage — The Forward Visibility Lens
cRPO ÷ NTM revenue estimates
RBC defines RPO coverage as current RPO (cRPO, the next-twelve-months portion) divided by the following four quarters of revenue. They use historicals rather than guidance for this calculation since SaaS companies tend to guide conservatively. A coverage ratio materially above historical norms implies the company may be sandbagging guidance — more upside potential. Both the deferred revenue % and RPO coverage are new disclosures enabled by ASC 606, and RBC calls them the only true positives of that standard (with some irony, noting ASC 606 "caused more confusion and made analyzing companies more difficult than before").

Part 1 — Calculated Billings: The Death of the Gold Standard

Calculated billings (revenue + change in deferred revenue) was the undisputed leading indicator for SaaS for years. No management disclosure needed — derive it entirely from GAAP. RBC outlines what made it dominant, and why it has lost relevance:

Why Billings Was the Gold Standard
  • 100% GAAP-derived: revenue from the income statement + deferred revenue from the balance sheet. No management judgment, no special disclosure, no non-GAAP reconciliation needed.
  • Forward-looking on a long-term basis: historically, a slowdown in billings foreshadowed a slowdown in revenue growth. It attempts to measure new business generated in the period, not revenue recognized.
  • Flexible variants — investors could adapt it:
    • Current/short-term billings: revenue + change in current deferred revenue only — controls for duration. A company shifting customers to multi-year deals inflates total billings without new volume; current billings is less affected.
    • Subscription billings: subscription revenue + current deferred — strips out professional services, isolates recurring quality.
    • TTM billings: trailing twelve-month view — smooths seasonal volatility. Companies like DocuSign, PagerDuty, and Slack emphasized this variant.
    • OCF-based billings: uses the change in deferred revenue from the cash flow statement rather than the balance sheet — controls for FX fluctuations that can distort the B/S balance.
Why Billings Has Become Unreliable
  • Duration sensitivity — the biggest flaw: when customers shift from annual to monthly or quarterly billing cycles (which happened at scale during COVID, especially with SMB customers), billings collapses even if the underlying contract value and business health are completely unchanged. The metric confuses billing frequency with business momentum.
  • Useless for consumption-based companies: businesses like Twilio and Fastly bill in arrears based on actual usage — there is no invoicing-ahead-of-delivery cycle for billings to capture. Change in deferred revenue is essentially zero. The metric is fundamentally incompatible with usage-based pricing.
  • Gameable — forces unnatural behavior: management teams have been caught offering discounts to close deals early (inflating a given quarter's billings), or discounting multi-year deals specifically to improve billings optics. RBC notes you can fix the FCF distortion from multi-year deals by calculating operating cash flow using only the change in current deferred revenue, excluding long-term deferred revenue from the calculation.
  • Volatile and seasonal: enterprise software is heavily Q4-weighted. Changes in renewal calendaring, early renewals, or co-terminus billings (a customer aligning contract end dates across all vendors) can create massive quarter-to-quarter swings that have nothing to do with business trajectory.
The Slack Case Study — Why Full RPO Disclosure Is Strategically Important

During COVID, Slack saw a notable billings deceleration as customers shifted to shorter payment terms. Slack had adopted the ASC 606 "practical expedient" — excluding contracts under 12 months from RPO — so they had no clean alternative metric to offer investors. They spent enormous time and management attention explaining why billings wasn't a good metric for their business. Without RPO to point to, investors remained skeptical. Revenue ultimately did slow, validating the billings signal retroactively and damaging credibility. The lesson: management teams that take the practical expedient trade away their ability to use RPO as a defensive tool when billings looks bad. Full RPO disclosure is strategically valuable precisely when it's most needed.

Billings Formula Variants

Calculated Billings = Revenue + (Ending Total Deferred Revenue − Beginning Total Deferred Revenue) Current (ST) Billings = Revenue + Change in Short-Term Deferred Revenue only → Better: controls for duration shifts; shorter-duration deals don't inflate this Subscription Billings = Subscription Revenue + Change in Current Deferred Revenue → Strips out services revenue; isolates high-quality recurring TTM Billings = Sum of trailing four quarters of calculated billings → Controls for quarterly seasonality and one-time renewal timing OCF-based Billings = Revenue + Change in Deferred Revenue from Cash Flow Statement (not B/S) → Controls for FX fluctuations on the balance sheet deferred balance

Part 2 — Remaining Performance Obligations (RPO): The Modern Standard

RPO is a GAAP metric introduced under ASC 606. It captures the total future contractual revenue a company is obligated to deliver — both the billed portion (sitting in deferred revenue on the balance sheet) and the unbilled portion (future obligations from signed contracts not yet invoiced, which never appeared anywhere under the old rules). Think of it as a comprehensive order backlog that's now required to be disclosed.

RBC notes that some companies historically disclosed off-balance-sheet backlog on an annual basis (BOX and ServiceNow were examples). Adding that historical backlog to total deferred revenue approximately equals what is now reported as RPO — useful for building RPO trend data that predates ASC 606's effective date.

RPO Formulas — The Full Framework

Total RPO = Billed backlog (deferred revenue on B/S) + Unbilled contractual backlog (future invoices not yet sent) → Represents all committed future revenue from existing contracts Current RPO (cRPO) = Portion of Total RPO expected to be recognized in the next 12 months (NTM) → Most companies use NTM; Workday uses next 24 months — less comparable cRPO Coverage = cRPO ÷ NTM Revenue (using historicals, not guidance) → Sector median: ~63–66% → Well above historical norm = potentially sandbagged guidance / upside → Below historical norm = higher reliance on new bookings to hit revenue targets Calculated Bookings = Revenue + Change in Total RPO → RPO-era billings replacement; captures full new contract value in the period Current Bookings = Revenue + Change in cRPO → Less volatile Q/Q than calculated bookings; preferred for momentum analysis

5 Ways RBC Uses RPO to Gauge Business Health

1. Total RPO Growth YoY
Is the total backlog expanding?
Growth in total RPO shows whether the company is building or depleting its contracted future revenue. An RPO acceleration typically leads a revenue growth acceleration by 2–4 quarters, since RPO must be recognized ratably over contract terms. BOX's FY22 revenue acceleration showed up in RPO before it appeared in billings.
2. cRPO Growth YoY
Best predictor of NTM revenue
Current RPO tracks specifically what's coming in the next twelve months. RBC considers this the most reliable single predictor of NTM revenue growth. Companies that guide directly to cRPO — Salesforce, ServiceNow, Workday — provide the clearest investor transparency and have successfully shifted attention away from billings.
3. cRPO Coverage Ratio
Guidance conservatism detector
Divide cRPO by NTM revenue estimates using historical (not guided) numbers. Materially above historical norms → likely sandbagged; below norms → acceleration must come from new bookings, not existing backlog. A coverage near historical average says guidance is fairly set.
4. Calculated Bookings
Revenue + Δ Total RPO
The full-RPO bookings metric. Captures the total annualized value of all contracts signed. More volatile quarter-to-quarter than billings because a single large multi-year enterprise renewal can spike the number. Best for annual analysis; noisier quarterly. Still superior to billings because multi-year deal value is captured at signing, not just when invoiced.
5. Current Bookings
Revenue + Δ cRPO
Near-term only — less volatile quarter-to-quarter than calculated bookings because it doesn't swing as wildly on large multi-year renewals. RBC prefers this for quarterly momentum analysis. When both calculated and current bookings are decelerating together, that's a stronger signal of slowdown than either alone.

The Practical Expedient Problem — When RPO Is Structurally Incomplete

ASC 606 allows companies to exclude from their RPO any contracts with an initial duration of 12 months or less — the "practical expedient." The intent was to reduce administrative burden, but the effect is that certain companies' RPO figures materially understate their true backlog, making the metric far less useful.

Companies That Apply the Practical Expedient (RPO Is Incomplete for These)

RBC flags: Coupa, DocuSign, Guidewire, MongoDB, NICE, PagerDuty, and Palantir — all exclude performance obligations from contracts ≤12 months. Duck Creek and Veeva apply it only to professional services contracts under 12 months. Workday excludes all professional services RPO entirely (regardless of duration). When reviewing RPO for these names, know you are seeing an understated figure — cross-reference with billings or ARR. For companies with full RPO disclosure who also guide to the metric, RPO is the most powerful tool available.

Part 3 — Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR): Non-GAAP, Context-Dependent

ARR was historically reserved for subscription-transition companies or used as a private company vanity metric. It has since expanded into three categories of legitimate public company use — but comes with meaningful limitations that require careful attention:

Three Legitimate Use Cases for ARR
  • Cloud-transition companies (Guidewire, Pegasystems, Splunk): ARR normalizes for the J-curve. During a perpetual-to-SaaS transition, GAAP revenue can decline or even go negative even as new contract value grows strongly — ARR cuts through that timing distortion and shows true recurring business health. RBC considers ARR essentially mandatory for properly evaluating transition-stage companies.
  • On-premise term license companies (UiPath, Alteryx): ASC 606 requires upfront recognition of the license component in term licenses, creating lumpy GAAP revenue even for stable businesses. ARR annualizes the contract value over its full term, reflecting the true recurring nature of these engagements.
  • Pure SaaS companies using ARR voluntarily (Dropbox, CrowdStrike): companies that prefer ARR as their primary leading indicator over billings — typically because variable billing durations make billings too noisy or because they believe ARR better reflects the trajectory of the business. Dropbox explicitly flags ARR as its primary KPI.
The Definition Problem — No Universal Standard

Every company defines ARR differently. RBC's most important practical warning: always read the footnotes before comparing ARR across companies. Key variation points across RBC's coverage:

  • CrowdStrike: annualized value of active subscription contracts at period end, assuming renewal even past expiry if in active discussions
  • Dropbox: active paid users × annualized subscription price; FX rates locked at annual update, not floating
  • Clearwater Analytics: last month's recurring revenue ÷ days in month × 365
  • Nutanix: ACV for all non-life-of-device contracts (separates subscription from LOD hardware-attached contracts)
  • Informatics/PingIdentity: MRR × 12, where MRR = total contract value ÷ contract months
  • Investor estimate when not disclosed: most recent quarterly subscription revenue × 4

Variation dimensions: does it include services? maintenance on perpetual licenses? Assume renewal on expiring contracts? Use fixed or floating FX? Include consumption minimums?

ARR Limitations — Four Reasons to Be Careful

1. No Standard Definition
Cross-company comparison is unreliable
RBC recommends "testing" disclosed ARR by comparing it to TTM or annualized billings, or to a calculated ARR estimate (quarterly subscription revenue × 4). If the company's ARR diverges materially from these checks, investigate the definition. The bigger the gap, the more aggressive or unusual the ARR methodology.
2. ARR Can Be Misleading
The DOMO example
DOMO disclosed $100M in ARR as a private company in early 2015 — but didn't reach $100M in TTM GAAP revenue until 2018, three years later. Why? DOMO defined ARR as an annualized billings run rate — significantly diverging from recognized revenue. If ARR is telling a substantially different story than GAAP revenue, that's a red flag about the definition, not evidence of accounting conservatism.
3. It's Non-GAAP — Methodology Can Change
No anchor to a GAAP standard
Unlike billings or RPO, ARR is not anchored to any GAAP requirement. Companies can redefine what's included, change when expiring contracts are dropped, or adjust FX treatment — making historical trend analysis unreliable if you're not tracking methodology changes. Always verify the current definition matches prior periods before drawing conclusions.
4. Most Companies Don't Guide to ARR
Hard to measure against expectations
Every public company CFO knows managing expectations is critical — but many companies disclose ARR without providing guidance for it. This limits its usefulness as a beat/miss metric. Companies that do guide to ARR (UiPath, Alteryx, Pegasystems) are more investor-friendly. RBC calls out wanting more companies to adopt ARR guidance as standard practice.
Companies That Would Benefit From ARR Disclosure (Per RBC)

RBC flags several companies that don't currently disclose ARR but would benefit from doing so: Zoom — to better quantify the magnitude of prosumer/SMB churn vs. enterprise strength. NICE — undergoing cloud transition with consumption-based billing in arrears, making standard metrics hard to read. MongoDB — Atlas (consumption) growing as share of revenue makes GAAP increasingly lumpy. Twilio, Fastly, New Relic, Elastic — all consumption-based where ARR or ACV could serve as a more stable forward indicator than usage billings.

Part 4 — Consumption Companies: None of the Three Work Well

For consumption-based businesses (Twilio, Snowflake, Fastly, MongoDB's Atlas, NICE's CCaaS, New Relic), all three standard metrics have fundamental limitations:

Why Standard Metrics Break Down
  • Billings is useless: consumption companies bill in arrears based on actual usage — there is no invoicing-ahead-of-delivery cycle. The change in deferred revenue is essentially zero, making calculated billings meaningless.
  • RPO dramatically understates future revenue: contracts only obligate customers to minimum commitments, but actual usage routinely runs 3–5× above minimums. RPO coverage for consumption companies is typically just 10–30%, not because the business is unhealthy, but because only the minimum is contractually committed. Exhibit 4 in the RBC report shows SNOW, AKAM, FSLY, NEWR, MDB, TWLO all with very low cRPO coverage.
  • ARR is directionally useful but flawed: annualizing trailing usage assumes flat future consumption. Consumption businesses are heavily seasonal — Fastly and Twilio see material holiday-driven spikes that would make a Q4 ARR run rate overstate the full-year picture. A Y/Y ARR growth rate would normalize for seasonality, but the absolute level can mislead.
RBC's Recommendation: Use NRR as the Primary Indicator

For consumption companies, Net Revenue Retention (NRR) is the best available leading indicator, as outlined in RBC's separate NRR whitepaper. Key points:

  • NRR improvement or deterioration reliably signals a coming acceleration or deceleration in revenue — especially meaningful for consumption models where usage patterns are more volatile
  • For consumption companies, NRR reflects actual usage expansion from existing customers, which is the core growth driver — making it more informative than any bookings-based metric
  • Twilio reports NRR quarterly, not TTM — RBC praises this specifically because it provides a more timely signal on usage trends than the lagged annual view
  • RBC is open to companies exploring ARR as a supplemental consumption disclosure but does not yet consider it best practice. The annualized trailing approach may work for companies with stable usage baselines and high retention, less so for pure usage-surge businesses.

OneStream & Kantata Context — How This Applies to Your Work

OneStream sells enterprise SaaS on multi-year subscription contracts — making the RPO framework the most directly relevant lens. When you process consulting orders in Kantata, each signed contract contributes to RPO at signing: the billed portion sits in deferred revenue on the balance sheet; the unbilled future invoices are off-balance-sheet backlog. Revenue is then recognized ratably as the performance obligation is delivered under ASC 606. The contract structure — term length, billing frequency, services vs. pure SaaS mix — determines which metric best captures that contract's contribution to forward revenue visibility. A multi-year SaaS deal is exactly the scenario where cRPO matters most to investors. A short consulting engagement might not even appear in RPO if the practical expedient is applied. Understanding these mechanics means you can read investor communications about OneStream's topline and understand exactly why they're choosing to highlight the metrics they do.
