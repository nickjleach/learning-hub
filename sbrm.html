Unprofitable ≠ Uninvestable — How Accounting Distorts Profit Views

Source: Needham Industry Update, April 26, 2022 — Priority: Crash course on company valuation
The Core Argument

Company valuation = PV of all future cash flows, NOT PV of future earnings. GAAP losses in SaaS are largely an accounting artifact driven by FASB rules that force software companies to expense growth investments (R&D, S&M headcount) as incurred — while companies in retail, industrials, and energy can capitalize those same investments. This creates an apples-to-oranges comparison.

The Walmart vs. Workday Case Study

Walmart (WMT) — Traditional Retail
  • GAAP profitable at IPO (Year 8)
  • Did NOT generate positive FCF until Year 34
  • Growth investments = store buildings (capitalized, not expensed)
  • CapEx peaked at 5% of revenue during growth years
  • FCF margins never surpassed 5% over its history
Workday (WDAY) — Subscription SaaS
  • Generated positive FCF in Year 10
  • Did NOT generate GAAP net income until Year 16
  • Growth investments = R&D + S&M headcount (expensed immediately)
  • FCF margins better than any year in WMT's history since 2015
  • SBC-adjusted FCF still outperformed WMT by 2021
Key Accounting Insight

If you expensed Walmart's CapEx as incurred (like FASB does for software R&D), WMT would have looked deeply unprofitable for its first 20+ years — yet no one would call WMT a bad business. The same logic applies in reverse to SaaS: forced in-period expensing makes SaaS look worse than it is during the growth phase.

Needham built a DCF model to show how EV/Revenue multiples work for SaaS. Key assumptions: 30% peak Non-GAAP operating margins at maturity, 80%+ gross margins, 8% discount rate, Rule of 40 governs growth-phase margins.

This is why investors pay enormous premiums for durable, high-growth SaaS companies.

ARR, Billings & RPO — Topline Leading Indicators

Reading Guidance

The RBC report covers these topline metrics in depth — the company-specific analyses are less important than the conceptual framework. The key concept: SaaS revenue is backward-looking. ARR, billings, and RPO are how investors look ahead. These are the primary leading indicators of future growth.

Why Leading Indicators Matter in SaaS

In a SaaS model, GAAP revenue is a lagging metric. Roughly 63% of a typical SaaS company's quarterly revenue was already sitting in deferred revenue at the start of the quarter — reflecting decisions customers made months or years ago, not current momentum. To gauge what's happening now, you need forward-looking signals.

Calculated Billings
Revenue + Δ Deferred Revenue
The legacy "gold standard" leading indicator. Derived entirely from GAAP line items — no management judgment required. Increasingly unreliable due to duration sensitivity, consumption model incompatibility, and potential for manipulation.
RPO (Remaining Performance Obligations)
Total committed future revenue
A GAAP metric introduced under ASC 606. Captures all future obligations from signed contracts — both billed (deferred revenue) and unbilled backlog. More comprehensive than billings because it includes multi-year contract backlog that never touches deferred revenue until invoiced.
ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue)
Annualized value of active subscriptions
A non-GAAP metric — each company defines it slightly differently. Most common: annualize the most recent quarter's recurring revenue (Q × 4). Most useful for cloud-transition companies and consumption-heavy businesses where GAAP revenue timing distorts the picture.

When to Use Each Metric

MetricGAAP?Best ForMain Weakness
Billings Derived from GAAP Pure-play SaaS with annual billing cycles and stable durations Sensitive to billing duration; useless for consumption companies; can be gamed
RPO / cRPO Yes Enterprise SaaS with multi-year contracts; companies that guide to RPO (CRM, NOW, WDAY) Some companies apply the ASC 606 "practical expedient" and exclude short contracts, making it incomplete
ARR No — non-GAAP Cloud-transition companies; consumption models; private companies at growth stage No standard definition — Snowflake, CrowdStrike, and Dropbox all calculate it differently

Key Formulas

Calculated Billings = Revenue + (Ending Deferred Revenue – Beginning Deferred Revenue) Current Billings (ST) = Revenue + Change in Short-Term Deferred Revenue only Total RPO = Billed backlog (deferred revenue) + Unbilled contractual backlog Current RPO (cRPO) = Portion of total RPO expected to be recognized in next 12 months RPO Coverage Ratio = cRPO ÷ NTM Revenue [RBC median across SaaS: ~63–66%] ARR (common estimate) = Most recent quarter's subscription revenue × 4 ARR (CrowdStrike) = Annualized value of active subscription contracts at period end ARR (Clearwater) = (Last month's recurring revenue ÷ days in month) × 365
The "Death of Billings"

Billings was the dominant SaaS metric for years because it could be calculated from standard GAAP financials without needing management disclosure. COVID exposed its fragility: customers shifted to monthly/quarterly billing cycles, collapsing billings figures even at healthy companies. ASC 606's introduction of RPO gave investors a better tool. Billings is now considered noisy, not conclusive — use RPO or cRPO for enterprise SaaS, and ARR for transition or consumption stories.

Source: Barclays, April 2022 — Great introduction to the North Star metric for software companies

The Rule of 40 is the dominant benchmark metric for evaluating whether a SaaS company is striking the right balance between growth and profitability. It recognizes that early-stage SaaS companies must trade off margin for growth.

The Rule of 40 is the primary benchmark used in investor conversations and company pitches. A company at Rule of 40 can justify its S&M and R&D spend. This is considered normal in SaaS — not a sign of a troubled business.

LTV / CAC — Customer Return on Investment

Source: Goldman Sachs, March 8, 2022 — One of the most important sales efficiency metrics

LTV/CAC measures the return on investment from acquiring a new customer — how much lifetime value does each customer generate relative to what it cost to acquire them?

LTV — Lifetime Value
Revenue × Gross Margin ÷ Churn Rate
The total gross profit expected from a customer over their lifetime. Higher gross margins and lower churn = higher LTV. This is why SaaS businesses obsess over net revenue retention.
CAC — Customer Acquisition Cost
S&M Spend ÷ New Customers Added
The total sales and marketing cost to acquire one new customer. Includes sales headcount, marketing programs, commissions. CAC payback = how long until a customer becomes profitable.
LTV/CAC Ratio
LTV / CAC = (Gross Margin % × ARPU) / Churn Rate ÷ S&M Spend / New Customers Added Simplified: Annual Contract Value × Gross Margin % × Customer Lifetime Years ÷ Cost to Acquire One Customer
LTV/CAC RatioInterpretation
> 3xExcellent — SaaS benchmark for a healthy, scalable business
1x – 3xAcceptable but room to improve — typically early-stage or in heavy growth investment
< 1xDestroying value — costs more to acquire a customer than they'll ever generate
Connection to Magic Number

Magic Number and LTV/CAC are both sales efficiency metrics but measure different things. Magic Number is a quick approximation using public financials (revenue growth ÷ S&M spend). LTV/CAC requires deeper data: churn rates, gross margins per customer, and acquisition costs broken out by channel. Together they triangulate the health of the go-to-market engine.

Magic Number — Sales Efficiency

Source: Baird Equity Research, April 26, 2022 — Quick intro to another good SaaS sales efficiency metric

The Magic Number measures how efficiently a company converts Sales & Marketing spend into recurring revenue. It approximates the revenue generated for every dollar spent on S&M in the preceding cycle.

Why It Matters

Magic Number helps you answer: "Should this company be investing more or less in sales?" A high number means each S&M dollar is generating outsized revenue — you should pour fuel on the fire. A low number means the go-to-market engine is inefficient and needs to be fixed before scaling.

Formula — Short Sales Cycles (< 90 days / PLG models)
Magic Number = (Quarterly Revenue_t - Quarterly Revenue_t-1) × 4 ÷ S&M Spend_t-1 → Then take trailing 4-quarter average
Formula — Long Sales Cycles (Enterprise, > 90 days)
Magic Number = (TTM Revenue_t - TTM Revenue_t-1) ÷ Prior Year S&M Spend

Baird revised the formula in 2022 because the original quarterly metric didn't account for longer enterprise sales cycles. For enterprise companies, the revenue impact of S&M investment takes longer to show up.

Magic NumberInterpretationAction Signal
> 1.0Highly efficient S&M — strong ROI on each dollar spentAggressively increase S&M investment
0.75 – 1.0Good efficiency — investment is paying offContinue investing; monitor trends
< 0.75Inefficient — more spent than revenue generatedFix S&M efficiency before scaling spend
Relationship to LTV/CAC

Magic Number is backwards-looking (what did past S&M spend produce?) while LTV/CAC is forward-looking (what is the expected lifetime value of a new customer?). The two metrics complement each other — use both together to get a full picture of sales engine health.

Why Companies Transition to SaaS

Source: Jefferies SaaS Transition, February 2022 — Explains why companies like to go to SaaS

Understanding the SaaS transition is foundational — it explains why software companies shifted away from perpetual licenses, and why the resulting accounting changes create the "unprofitable" optics that Needham's analysis debunks above.

For Vendors
Predictable, Recurring Revenue
Subscription revenue recognized ratably smooths lumpy perpetual license deals. Predictable ARR enables better planning, supports higher valuation multiples, and reduces reliance on year-end deal closings.
For Customers
Lower Upfront Cost, Always Current
No large upfront CapEx — OpEx model. Always on the latest version. Easier to expand usage. Exit costs are lower (though this cuts both ways for vendor retention).
Long-Term Business Quality
Compounding Renewal Economics
Renewals require no incremental S&M cost. As the installed base grows, renewals become a larger share of revenue. Net Revenue Retention above 100% = organic growth from existing customers alone.
The Accounting Consequence of SaaS

Under legacy perpetual license models, revenue was front-loaded (recognized when software was delivered). Under SaaS, revenue is recognized ratably over the contract term. This means a company switching from perpetual to SaaS will see apparent revenue decline during transition — even though underlying bookings and customer value may be growing. This is not a business deterioration; it's an accounting timing shift. Investors must look at ARR and Billings, not just GAAP revenue, to see what's happening.

ASC 606 — Revenue Recognition Mechanics

Source: ASC 606 Summary — Quick intro to how bookings / billings turn into revenue

ASC 606 is the FASB revenue recognition standard that replaced ASC 605. The key change for software companies: term licenses are no longer recognized ratably — the license element is front-loaded upon delivery, while maintenance/support is spread over the contract period. This matters for modeling because it accelerates revenue recognition for certain deal types.

Commission Expense Changes Under ASC 606

Sales commissions are now capitalized and amortized over the estimated customer life (~5 years) rather than expensed in the period of the deal. This improves near-term margins but creates a deferred commission asset on the balance sheet.

OneStream & Kantata Context

OneStream sells enterprise SaaS on multi-year subscription contracts — meaning revenue is recognized ratably under ASC 606, not front-loaded. When you process consulting orders in Kantata, each signed contract contributes to RPO at signing: the billed portion sits in deferred revenue on the balance sheet; the unbilled future invoices are off-balance-sheet backlog. A multi-year SaaS deal is exactly the scenario where cRPO matters most to investors. A short consulting engagement might not even appear in RPO if the practical expedient is applied — understanding this distinction helps you read investor communications about OneStream's topline with more context.

Jefferies — No Pain, No Gain: The SaaS Transition Playbook

Source: Jefferies Equity Research, February 28, 2022 — Charles Brennan & Alex Nguyen
Context

Categorized under Accounting Knowledge — specifically to explain why companies transition to SaaS. But the Jefferies analysis goes deeper: it also documents the financial J-curve (the "pain") and the long-term valuation re-rating (the "gain"), making it essential for investor conversations about any software vendor mid-transition.

What Is a SaaS Transition?

Jefferies identifies two simultaneous changes when a software company goes SaaS:

1. Architectural Shift
  • Software moves from on-premise to cloud-hosted — managed by the vendor, not the customer
  • Vendor controls updates, uptime, and version management
  • All customers on the same version — enabling faster product innovation cycles
  • Drives operational gains: scalability, flexibility, cross-sell, infrastructure monetization
2. Contracting Shift
  • From licence + maintenance (large upfront fee + ~20% annual maintenance) to subscription (bundled, recognized ratably)
  • Revenue recognition pushed to the right — same total value, spread over years instead of front-loaded
  • Creates the J-curve: near-term revenue compression, eventual acceleration as subscription base compounds
  • Management rarely cuts costs during the revenue dip — causing temporary margin compression

The 5 Operational Gains

1. Scalability
Cloud scales with demand
No need to buy infrastructure for peak capacity. Elastic compute is more cost-efficient than on-prem hardware provisioning.
2. Flexibility
Access from anywhere
Cloud enables remote work, mobile access, and easier collaboration — directly improving customer satisfaction and stickiness.
3. Automatic Updates
Vendor manages versions
All customers on the same version = faster feature delivery, fewer support tickets for legacy bugs, more innovation cycles per year.
4. Cross-Selling
Seamless expansion
Cloud delivery makes it frictionless for customers to adopt additional modules, driving higher NRR and revenue per customer.
5. Infrastructure Revenue
Capture hosting costs
Vendors can charge holistically for the solution including infrastructure. They use hyperscalers (AWS/Azure/GCP) at volume discounts — the spread is incremental low-risk revenue.

The Quantified Gain — Jefferies' Cross-Company Data

Jefferies studied 4 companies that completed SaaS transitions (ADSK, PTC, ADBE, INTU) plus 4 mid-transition (SAP, Sage, Software AG, CyberArk). Key results from the completers:

Revenue Growth Acceleration
+6 percentage points
Average acceleration in annual revenue growth rate post-transition. Growth re-accelerates once subscription base compounds and licence drag disappears.
Margin Improvement
+250 bps avg. EBIT
Recurring revenue provides operating leverage — cost base becomes more fixed relative to revenue. Compared to pre-transition adj. EBIT margins.
Valuation Re-Rating
+~3x EV/NTM Revenue
All 4 studied US completers saw meaningful multiple expansion 2 years after margins inflected. Market rewards recurring revenue quality and visible growth.
Average Transition Duration
3.7 years average
Range: ADSK took only 1 year; INTU took nearly 6 years. Depends on product complexity, starting mix of recurring revenue, and how aggressively management pushes the migration.

The Cost — The J-Curve in Detail

Revenue Impact
  • Near-term revenue growth falls by ~10% as licence revenue (front-loaded) gives way to ratable subscription revenue
  • In extreme cases (like Adobe in 2013), GAAP revenue can go negative year-over-year during a fast transition
  • This is an accounting artifact — ARR and bookings may be growing strongly throughout
  • Once licence revenue shrinks to ~10-15%, subscription compounding takes over and growth re-accelerates
Margin Impact
  • Companies increase opex by ~5% on average to invest in cloud infrastructure and retrain sales for subscription GTM
  • Average margin compression: ~15% from peak to trough
  • Average quarters to margin trough: ~6 quarters
  • Average quarters to return to pre-transition margin level: ~9 quarters
  • Total foregone profit: average ~1.5 years of pre-transition profit
Key Inflection Point: ~75-80% Recurring Revenue

Once a company reaches roughly 75–80% recurring revenue as a share of total revenue, Jefferies found that margins typically start inflecting upward. At this point, legacy licence revenue is small enough that it no longer drags on reported growth, and the subscription base's compounding economics dominate. This is the signal investors watch for when evaluating mid-transition companies.

As of February 2022, Jefferies noted that all 4 completed US transitions resulted in positive valuation re-ratings, but there was no evidence of valuation upside in Europe. SAP, Sage, and Software AG were all still trading at discounts to their pre-transition levels. Jefferies speculated this may reflect different investor bases, market depth, or product complexity factors — but it was still unresolved.

OneStream Context

OneStream is a native cloud SaaS company — it never went through a painful perpetual-to-subscription transition. This is a competitive advantage: OneStream entered the market with all the operational benefits of SaaS without the J-curve penalty that legacy ERPs like SAP and Oracle are still navigating. The subscription-based ARR from consulting contracts processed in Kantata is the revenue engine that Jefferies identifies as structurally superior to perpetual licensing.

Goldman Sachs — Benchmarking LTV:CAC Across the Software Sector

Source: Goldman Sachs Equity Research, March 8, 2022 — Gabriela Borges, Kash Rangan et al.
What to Focus On

The Goldman report covers all major software KPIs for Q4 2021. The key takeaways: (1) how LTV and CAC are formally defined and calculated, (2) the broader market context shift from "growth at any cost" to "profitable growth," and (3) how LTV:CAC correlates to EV/Sales multiples.

Goldman's Formal LTV:CAC Definitions

LTV Formula (Goldman)
LTV = GAAP Subscription Gross Margin % ÷ (Gross Revenue Churn % + 8.5% discount rate) Key inputs: • Gross margin: cost to service $1 of recurring revenue • Gross churn: how fast the customer base decays • 8.5% discount rate: reflects long-term displacement/ disruption risk inherent in software

The 8.5% discount rate acknowledges that even great SaaS businesses face competitive risk over time. LTV is the discounted present value of $1 of recurring revenue.

CAC Formula (Goldman)
CAC = TTM GAAP S&M Expense ÷ (Gross New ARR) Where: • GAAP S&M includes stock-based compensation (Goldman views SBC as a real cost of doing business) • Gross New ARR = YoY change in ARR + gross churn (cost to add $1 of gross new ARR, before churn)

Using gross new ARR (not net) is deliberate — it isolates the new logo acquisition cost from the churn offset, giving a cleaner view of sales engine efficiency.

Q4 2021 Market Context

The Rate Shift
Growth at any cost is over
Rising interest rates in 2022 compressed software multiples back to 2019 levels. Investors shifted focus from pure revenue growth to quality of growth — specifically, unit economics and FCF generation.
Correlation Shift
LTV:CAC ↑, Revenue Growth ↓
In Q4 2021, the correlation of EV/Sales multiples with LTV:CAC increased while the correlation with revenue growth decreased. The market was rewarding efficiency, not just speed.
Median LTV:CAC
4.06x (down from 4.17x)
The slight decline was driven by CAC rising (S&M spend normalizing after COVID tailwinds). Goldman expected further CAC increases in 2022 from in-person events resuming and wage inflation.

RBC — ARR, Billings & RPO: A Complete Framework

Source: RBC Capital Markets Software Primers, April 28, 2022
Reading Guidance

Focus on the topline metrics framework — the company-specific analyses in the original report are less critical. The key concept: SaaS revenue is backward-looking. ARR, billings, and RPO are how investors see what's coming before it hits the income statement.

Part 1 — Billings: The Legacy Metric

Billings (Revenue + change in deferred revenue) was the dominant SaaS metric for years. It requires no management disclosure — derivable from standard GAAP financials. Its advantage is objectivity; its weakness is sensitivity.

Why Billings Worked
  • Purely GAAP-derived — no management judgment required
  • Captures new invoicing activity in the period regardless of revenue recognition timing
  • Effective when all companies bill annually and have similar contract durations
  • Easy to compare across companies using only public filings
Why Billings Broke Down
  • COVID caused customers to shift to monthly/quarterly billing — collapsing billings at healthy companies
  • Duration changes distort the metric without reflecting any change in business quality
  • Completely useless for consumption-based businesses (Snowflake, Twilio) that bill in arrears
  • Can be gamed: pre-billing customers inflates billings without reflecting real demand

Part 2 — RPO: The GAAP Upgrade

Remaining Performance Obligations (RPO) was introduced under ASC 606 as a GAAP-required disclosure. It captures all signed contractual revenue not yet recognized — both the billed portion (deferred revenue) and the unbilled backlog from multi-year contracts.

cRPO (Current RPO)
Next 12 months of contracted revenue
The portion of total RPO expected to be recognized in the next 12 months. This is the most actionable metric — it's a near-term revenue floor derived from signed contracts, not forecasted demand.
RPO Coverage Ratio
cRPO ÷ NTM Revenue
RBC's preferred measure of forward revenue visibility. Sector median is ~63–66%. Companies above this range have more revenue already contracted than peers — a positive signal. Companies guiding to RPO (CRM, NOW, WDAY) can be benchmarked against this guidance.
Practical Expedient — Key Exception
Some companies opt out of RPO disclosure
ASC 606 allows companies to exclude contracts of 12 months or less from RPO disclosure (the "practical expedient"). Companies using this — including COUP, DOCU, GWRE, MDB, NICE, PD, PLTR — have RPO figures that systematically understate their true backlog.

Part 3 — ARR: Non-GAAP But Often Most Useful

When ARR Is Most Useful
  • Cloud-transition companies: GAAP revenue distorted by the J-curve; ARR shows what the business actually looks like in subscription terms
  • Private companies at growth stage: ARR is the primary metric for investor communications pre-IPO
  • Consumption companies: directionally useful as a trailing usage run rate, though seasonality requires Y/Y comparison
  • When management provides ARR guidance — enables beat/miss analysis alongside revenue
The Definition Problem — No Universal Standard

Every company defines ARR differently. Always read the footnotes before comparing ARR across companies. Key variation points:

  • CrowdStrike: annualized value of active subscription contracts at period end, assuming renewal even past expiry if in active discussions
  • Dropbox: active paid users × annualized subscription price; FX rates locked at annual update
  • Clearwater Analytics: last month's recurring revenue ÷ days in month × 365
  • Investor estimate (when not disclosed): most recent quarterly subscription revenue × 4

Part 4 — Consumption Companies: None of the Three Work Well

For consumption-based businesses (Twilio, Snowflake, Fastly, MongoDB Atlas, New Relic), all three standard metrics have fundamental limitations:

Why Standard Metrics Break Down
  • Billings is useless: consumption companies bill in arrears based on actual usage — the change in deferred revenue is essentially zero, making calculated billings meaningless
  • RPO dramatically understates future revenue: contracts only obligate customers to minimum commitments, but actual usage routinely runs 3–5× above minimums. RPO coverage for consumption companies is typically just 10–30%
  • ARR is directionally useful but flawed: annualizing trailing usage assumes flat future consumption. Consumption businesses are heavily seasonal — a Q4 ARR run rate may overstate the full-year picture
RBC's Recommendation: Use NRR

For consumption companies, Net Revenue Retention (NRR) is the best available leading indicator:

  • NRR improvement or deterioration reliably signals a coming acceleration or deceleration in revenue
  • Reflects actual usage expansion from existing customers — the core growth driver in consumption models
  • Twilio reports NRR quarterly (not TTM) — providing a more timely signal on usage trends
  • RBC is open to ARR as a supplemental consumption disclosure but does not yet consider it best practice

OneStream & Kantata Context

OneStream sells enterprise SaaS on multi-year subscription contracts — making the RPO framework the most directly relevant lens. When you process consulting orders in Kantata, each signed contract contributes to RPO at signing: the billed portion sits in deferred revenue on the balance sheet; the unbilled future invoices are off-balance-sheet backlog. Revenue is then recognized ratably as the performance obligation is delivered under ASC 606. The contract structure — term length, billing frequency, services vs. pure SaaS mix — determines which metric best captures that contract's contribution to forward revenue visibility. A multi-year SaaS deal is exactly the scenario where cRPO matters most to investors.
